# Strategy Guardrails — Good vs Bad Strategy and Effort Cuts

## Overview

This document provides the strategic framework for the Operator → Owner transition. It is grounded in Richard Rumelt's *Good Strategy/Bad Strategy* logic and tailored to Jaye's dual-system reality: ThomCo (cash engine) and Venturr (leverage build).

---

## Good Strategy vs Bad Strategy — Tailored to You

### What Good Strategy Looks Like Right Now

Good strategy is a coherent response to an obstacle. It has three components: a diagnosis of the challenge, a guiding policy for dealing with it, and a set of coherent actions that execute the policy.

**Diagnosis:** Your binding constraint is not ambition. It is **attention plus interruption load**. Operator demands from ThomCo consume the time and cognitive bandwidth needed for architect work on Venturr. The system depends on you as the default decision-maker for every routine issue, which means your capacity is the bottleneck for both businesses simultaneously.

**Guiding Policy:** Stabilise ThomCo to reduce volatility while protecting Venturr build blocks as non-negotiable. This means accepting that ThomCo growth is paused in favour of ThomCo stability, so that Venturr build time is protected.

**Coherent Actions:**
1. Standardise repeat decisions into a playbook so they stop requiring your involvement.
2. Batch communications into two daily windows so your day is not punctured continuously.
3. Reduce client and job complexity by enforcing minimum margin rules and firing the bottom 10% client type.
4. Delegate one function every 2–3 weeks with a written SOP so the machine learns to operate without you.

This is a coherent response to the actual obstacle. It is not a wishlist.

### What Bad Strategy Looks Like Right Now

Bad strategy is characterised by fluff, failure to face the problem, mistaking goals for strategy, and setting objectives without coherent action.

**Bad strategy for you right now includes:**

- **Trying to scale ThomCo and build Venturr aggressively at the same time** without first reducing operator noise. This ignores the binding constraint (your attention is finite) and treats ambition as a substitute for structure.

- **Declaring goals without installing the systems to make them inevitable.** Saying "I want to launch Venturr by Q3" without protecting build blocks, reducing operator load, and delegating functions is a goal without coherent action. It will fail not because of lack of effort but because of lack of structure.

- **Adding complexity when the system needs simplification.** New tools, new processes, new features, new client types — all of these increase operator load. The correct move is subtraction, not addition.

- **Confusing motion with progress.** Working 14-hour days across both businesses feels productive but produces less output than 6 focused hours with protected blocks. Busyness is not strategy.

## Ten Concrete Actions to Cut 20% Effort

These are designed as **cuts**, not improvements. Each one removes work rather than adding it. Pick the ones that apply immediately. Track them in the Monthly Measurement database under "Cuts Implemented".

### Cut 1: Stop Quoting Outside Your Target Job Type (30 days)
**What:** For the next 30 days, decline or deprioritise any quote request that falls outside your most profitable, most repeatable job type.
**Why:** Custom work creates disproportionate admin, rework, and decision overhead. Focusing on your target type reduces quoting time, improves margins, and makes scheduling predictable.
**Effort saved:** Estimated 3–5 hours per week in quoting, follow-up, and custom problem-solving.

### Cut 2: Introduce a Minimum Margin / Minimum Job Size Rule
**What:** Set a hard floor — any job below a defined margin percentage or dollar value gets declined or referred out.
**Why:** Small, low-margin jobs consume the same admin overhead as large ones but produce a fraction of the return. They are the silent killers of operator bandwidth.
**Effort saved:** Estimated 2–4 hours per week in admin, scheduling, and site management for low-return work.

### Cut 3: Batch Client Communications to Two Daily Windows
**What:** Handle all non-emergency client communications in two windows: 12:30 PM and 4:30 PM. Everything else waits.
**Why:** Continuous communication fragments your attention and makes deep work impossible. Batching is the single most effective time-management technique for operators.
**Effort saved:** Estimated 1–2 hours per day in context-switching and reactive communication.

### Cut 4: One Template for Proposals and Quotes
**What:** Create a single, reusable template for your most common proposal/quote type. Fill in the variables; send.
**Why:** Every custom proposal is rework. A template reduces quoting time from 30–60 minutes to 10–15 minutes.
**Effort saved:** Estimated 2–3 hours per week in proposal preparation.

### Cut 5: One Standard Materials Ordering Checklist
**What:** Create a single checklist for your most common job type that lists every material, quantity, and supplier. Use it every time.
**Why:** Ad-hoc ordering creates supplier back-and-forth, delays, and errors. A checklist eliminates the thinking step.
**Effort saved:** Estimated 1–2 hours per week in ordering, follow-up, and error correction.

### Cut 6: Fire Your Bottom 10% Client Type
**What:** Identify the client profile that creates the most friction (late payments, scope creep, excessive communication, unreasonable expectations) and stop accepting work from that profile.
**Why:** The bottom 10% of clients consume a disproportionate share of your time and energy. Removing them frees capacity for better clients and reduces stress.
**Effort saved:** Estimated 3–5 hours per week in friction management, plus significant energy recovery.

### Cut 7: No Site Decisions by Phone Unless Photo/Video First
**What:** Require that any site issue requiring your decision includes a photo or video before you engage. No photo, no decision.
**Why:** Phone-only site decisions are ambiguous, time-consuming, and often lead to misunderstanding. Visual context reduces decision time and error rate.
**Effort saved:** Estimated 1–2 hours per week in clarification calls and rework from misunderstood decisions.

### Cut 8: Move Small Admin to Friday Only
**What:** All non-urgent administrative tasks (filing, minor emails, scheduling adjustments, non-critical follow-ups) are batched to Friday afternoon.
**Why:** Daily admin drip fragments every day. Batching it to one session eliminates the constant low-level distraction.
**Effort saved:** Estimated 30–60 minutes per day (2.5–5 hours per week) in context-switching and admin interruption.

### Cut 9: One Weekly Bottleneck Kill Target
**What:** Each week, identify the single biggest bottleneck causing repeat problems and commit to eliminating it. Track it in Weekly Calibration.
**Why:** Working on everything at once means nothing gets fixed. One bottleneck per week creates compounding improvement — after 13 weeks, you have eliminated 13 sources of friction.
**Effort saved:** Compounds over time — each eliminated bottleneck permanently reduces future effort.

### Cut 10: Set Office Hours for Staff Escalation
**What:** Define two daily windows for staff escalation (e.g., 7:15–7:30 AM and 3:30–3:45 PM). Outside those windows, staff handle decisions themselves unless it is a safety or cash emergency.
**Why:** Continuous staff escalation is the primary mechanism by which your day gets punctured. Office hours force the team to batch their questions and attempt solutions first.
**Effort saved:** Estimated 1–3 hours per day in ad-hoc staff interruptions.

## Implementation Priority

Not all cuts need to happen at once. Here is the recommended sequence aligned to the 90-day transition plan:

| Week | Cut to Implement | Phase |
|---|---|---|
| Week 1–2 | Cut 3 (Batch comms) + Cut 10 (Office hours) | Stabilise |
| Week 3–4 | Cut 4 (Quote template) + Cut 5 (Ordering checklist) | Stabilise |
| Week 5–6 | Cut 1 (Stop quoting outside target) + Cut 2 (Minimum margin) | Systemise |
| Week 7–8 | Cut 6 (Fire bottom 10% client) + Cut 7 (Photo-first decisions) | Systemise |
| Week 9–10 | Cut 8 (Friday admin) + Cut 9 (Weekly bottleneck kill) | Step-back |

## Guardrail Questions

Use these questions as a weekly check against bad strategy:

1. **Am I adding or subtracting?** If the answer is adding, challenge whether it is necessary.
2. **Does this serve the ONE Thing or distract from it?** If it does not serve the lead domino, it waits.
3. **Am I confusing motion with progress?** Hours worked is not a metric. Outputs shipped is.
4. **Would I make this decision if I had half the time?** If not, it is probably not essential.
5. **Is this a system fix or a one-off patch?** Prefer systems. Patches create future work.
